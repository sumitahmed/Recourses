What is a Schedule?

A schedule in a DBMS is an arrangement of the operations of multiple transactions in a sequence, specifying the order in which they will be executed. Schedules are crucial for concurrency control as they determine whether the execution of concurrent transactions will maintain data consistency and avoid conflicts.

Real-Life Analogy:

Imagine a multi-lane toll booth where each vehicle (transaction) has to pass through its lane sequentially. If two vehicles in different lanes (transactions) interact or overlap incorrectly, it can cause a traffic jam (conflict). Similarly, a schedule in DBMS manages transaction order to ensure data consistency and avoid conflicts.

Types of Schedules

There are two main types of schedules in DBMS:

  • Serial Schedules: Transactions are executed one after another, with no interleaving. This ensures that each transaction completes before the next one begins, eliminating any possibility of conflict.
  • Non-Serial (Interleaved) Schedules: Operations of transactions are interleaved. While this increases concurrency and efficiency, it also creates the potential for conflicts if not managed carefully.

1. Serial Schedule

In a serial schedule, transactions execute one at a time in a strict sequence. Since there is no interleaving, serial schedules are conflict-free and always yield consistent results.

Example of Serial Schedule:

Consider two transactions:

  • T1: Withdraws $50 from Account A.
  • T2: Deposits $100 into Account A.

A serial schedule could be:

  • Execute all operations of T1 first.
  • Execute all operations of T2 afterward.

2. Non-Serial Schedule

In a non-serial schedule, operations from different transactions are interleaved. While this increases concurrency, it can also lead to conflicts or inconsistencies if not managed carefully.

Example of Non-Serial Schedule:

Consider the same transactions T1 and T2:

  • T1 starts and withdraws $50 from Account A.
  • T2 starts and deposits $100 into Account A.
  • T1 completes the remaining operations.

Conflict vs. View Serializability

Serializability is a property of schedules that ensures the outcome of a non-serial schedule is equivalent to some serial schedule. There are two main types of serializability:

1. Conflict Serializability

A schedule is conflict-serializable if it can be transformed into a serial schedule by rearranging non-conflicting operations. Conflict serializability is stricter than view serializability and often easier to check.

2. View Serializability

A schedule is view-serializable if it produces the same final database state as a serial schedule, even if conflicts are present. View serializability is more flexible but harder to achieve and check compared to conflict serializability.

Recoverable and Non-Recoverable Schedules

A schedule is recoverable if a transaction can undo or rollback without causing inconsistencies. Recoverable schedules ensure that transactions dependent on uncommitted data do not proceed until the dependent transaction commits.

Example of Recoverable and Non-Recoverable Schedules:

Consider transactions T1 and T2:

  • T1 writes to X and T2 reads X afterward.
  • If T1 commits, the schedule is recoverable.
  • If T1 is aborted and T2 has read uncommitted data, the schedule is non-recoverable.

Cascading Rollbacks and Cascadeless Schedules

When a transaction is rolled back, it may trigger a cascade of rollbacks in other transactions that depend on its uncommitted data. This is known as a cascading rollback.

Cascadeless Schedules

A cascadeless schedule prevents cascading rollbacks by ensuring that a transaction can only read data from committed transactions. This prevents dependent transactions from needing to rollback if the original transaction fails.

Example of Cascading Rollbacks and Cascadeless Schedule:

  • Cascading Rollback: T1 writes to X, T2 reads X, and then T1 aborts, forcing T2 to rollback as well.
  • Cascadeless Schedule: T2 waits until T1 commits before reading X, avoiding a rollback even if T1 aborts.
